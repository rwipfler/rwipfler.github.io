Research themes

My work sits at the intersection of microbial ecology, biogeochemistry, and deep-sea technology. Connecting these threads is one core motivation: the microbes living at methane seeps are doing chemistry that matters for global carbon cycling, but we've historically had to infer their activity from samples collected and depressurized on the way to the surface. I'm trying to close that gap.

In-situ measurement
Measuring what microbes do — at depth, under pressure

The activity of deep-sea microbes changes dramatically when you bring samples to the surface. My dissertation work centers on measuring microbial metabolic rates in situ — using custom high-pressure incubation systems deployed directly from research vessels and recovered with the pressure intact. The goal is a more honest picture of what is actually happening at the seafloor.

Methane seep biogeochemistry
Microbial communities that eat methane before it reaches the ocean

At cold seeps, consortia of archaea and bacteria perform anaerobic oxidation of methane — consuming a potent greenhouse gas before it can escape to the water column. I study the diversity, activity, and spatial organization of these communities at seeps along the California and Aleutian margins, and in methane-rich salt marsh sediments.

Tools & visualization
Making the seafloor legible — tools for communicating deep-ocean data

Field data from the deep sea is rich and multidimensional but hard to communicate in real time. As part of a cross-disciplinary collaboration, I contributed to DeepSee — a system for multidimensional visualization of seabed ecosystems, presented at CHI 2024. The thread connecting this to everything else: the deep ocean is hard to see, and that difficulty shapes what science gets done.

Outreach & science communication

Sharing deep-sea science with broader audiences has been part of my work since my first Nautilus cruise in 2017. The deep ocean is remote, expensive to access, and genuinely strange — which makes it both a challenge and an opportunity for public communication.

Live science · E/V Nautilus

Ship-to-Shore broadcasts

Participated in live Ship-to-Shore broadcasts during E/V Nautilus operations — real-time science communication connecting seafloor ROV dives with student audiences and the general public via the Nautilus Live platform.

Mentorship · Ocean Exploration Trust

Training the next cohort

As Science Manager in Training on E/V Nautilus, supervised and mentored ocean science interns through the same kind of field exposure that shaped my own career — coordinating science operations, running ROV deployments, and building the technical and professional skills needed for independent fieldwork.

Science communication

Talks & public engagement

Available for public talks, classroom visits, and science communication on deep-sea microbiology, methane cycling, and life in extreme environments. Particularly interested in reaching audiences who don't typically see themselves as part of the scientific community.
