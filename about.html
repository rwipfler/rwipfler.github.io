The scientist

I'm a fifth-year PhD candidate in the Orphan Lab at Caltech's Division of Geological and Planetary Sciences, working on the biogeochemistry of deep-sea methane seeps. My dissertation asks a deceptively simple question: what are the microbes actually doing down there? Answering it has meant building custom high-pressure incubation systems and deploying them from research vessels — measuring microbial metabolic activity in situ, at depth, in the dark, on the seafloor where pressure alone would crush most conventional gear.

Before Caltech, I studied Molecular and Cellular Biology at the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign, where I worked with Dr. Rachel Whitaker on extremophile archaea in Yellowstone hydrothermal springs — backcountry sample collection for the NASA Astrobiology Institute. That work convinced me that the most interesting microbiology was happening in the least hospitable places on earth, and it set the trajectory for everything since.

I'm also an Ocean Exploration Trust alumna, having joined E/V Nautilus first as an ocean science intern and later as Science Manager in Training — coordinating operations, managing ROV deployments, and supervising the next cohort of students through exactly the kind of field experience that shaped my own scientific direction.

I am supported by an NSF Graduate Research Fellowship (2020–2025). I've descended to the seafloor in HOV Alvin twice, and logged roughly 110 days at sea across 12 expeditions spanning Alaska, California, Mexico, Hawaii, American Samoa, and the Pacific Northwest.

Beyond the lab

When I'm not at sea or debugging pressure systems, I collect crushed cups — the Styrofoam ones that researchers decorate and send to depth on ROVs, which come back miniaturized and slightly uncanny. I once built a full-scale giant isopod Halloween costume and wore it to MBARI, where I was photographed alongside actual giant isopods. I'm drawn to artists who think about the deep ocean seriously — biodiversity illustrators, makers of strange marine objects — and I keep their work around me in the lab as a reminder that science and wonder are not separate things.
